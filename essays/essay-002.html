If it works, it works...

April 2026

A sigh of relief from a corner of the room. It worked. It almost always does, maybe not overnight, and perhaps never the way you expected it to. But in that moment, comes the big question - time to stop? Is it done? Or do you keep going - trying to make it better, faster, more efficient, more elegant?

Then you start weighing the risks and rewards. Is it worth risking the breaking of something that works, in the hope of making it better? Or do you let it be what it is - a little imperfect than your perfect idea? More often that not, conversations are only around enhancing, optimizing, or refining. Rarely does our first instinct make us stop to ask the quieter question - is it meaningful, or does it just work?

The drift

What I fear the most is when in these iterations, the original problem being left behind. Yes, you're working, still improving, but was it the same thing you set out to solve? Or are we now only focused on coming up with better solutions? What slips away in the process? The realization that - stakeholders are real, their requirements are real - but somehow, the actual need seems to get burnt under the weight of the next best thing.

Somewhere between the problem and the solution, the idea evolved.

The jolt

The realization is never gradual - doesn't creep in politely. It just hits - arrives all at once, slightly hinting at the fact that maybe, just maybe, what you've been building and what you actually needed are no longer on the same track. And the hardest part is - that drift is only visible from the other side, not when it's actually happening.

And that brings the tension - the discomfort of of knowing you're off track, but also not knowing what the right track looks like. So the plan now - get back to the wihteboard, reset, ask if the problem itself has shifted - because maybe it has, and then the drift isn't a drift at all, but an evolution - an answer to a question nobody asked, but perhaps should have.

And when it works...

Sometimes what looked like losing the thread was the thread finding a better path. The problem evolved, the solution followed. That's not a mistake, that's just how ideas move when you let them. Sometimes it looks like a slow wander away from what mattered toward what looked good.

Either way, you end up somewhere - something that works. Something real, something tested. And once you're there, the only thing left is to look at it honestly - what held, what didn't, what might crack under pressure you haven't seen yet.

Not to tear it down. Just to understand it. Because if it works, it works - and that's worth knowing exactly why.
