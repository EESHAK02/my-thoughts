Almost There, Always

March 2026

And yet again, the finish line moved.

It happens quietly enough that you almost miss it. You spend weeks getting comfortable with something — a model, a framework, a way of thinking about a problem — and just as the ground starts to feel solid, three new releases land in a single week and the ground is somewhere else entirely. Not gone. Just relocated, again, slightly further than where you were standing.

The pace

There's a particular rhythm to this field now. A model ships, and before anyone has finished writing the long-form analysis of what it means, an agent framework built on top of it ships too. Then a benchmark gets beaten. Then someone points out the benchmark didn't measure the right thing to begin with. Then a new benchmark appears. None of this is dramatic on its own. It's the accumulation that does something to your sense of time — the feeling that understanding something fully is no longer really the goal, because by the time you would, it's already one layer behind.

It's tempting to call this exhausting, and some days it is. But it's also just the texture of the thing right now. Less a series of events, more a weather system — you don't really expect it to stop, you just learn to dress for it.

A short history of retrieval

Take retrieval-augmented generation, since it's a clean example of how fast a "solved" idea can stop being solved. It started simply enough — chunk a document, embed it, store it, and when a question comes in, find the chunks that sit closest to it in vector space and hand them to a model. Blind similarity, essentially. No understanding of structure, no sense of how one chunk related to another. It worked well enough to feel like an answer.

And then, almost immediately, it stopped being just one thing. Hybrid retrieval arrived, blending keyword search back in because pure similarity was missing things that mattered. Graph-based approaches arrived, because flat chunks couldn't capture the relationships between ideas scattered across a document, or across many documents. Agentic retrieval arrived, where the system doesn't just fetch and hand over — it reasons about whether what it found is actually sufficient, and goes looking again if it isn't.

The same three letters, RAG, now describe a dozen different ways of doing a dozen different things.

And somewhere in the middle of all that branching, a quieter question started circulating — does retrieval, in its original sense, even hold the same place anymore? Context windows grew large enough that some of what RAG existed to solve simply stopped being a problem for certain use cases. The need didn't disappear. It just kept finding new shapes to live in.

The middle, permanently

What's strange is that none of this resolves into a clean before-and-after. There's no point where RAG was simple and now it's complicated, full stop. There's just an ongoing widening — more approaches, more combinations, more names for things that are subtly different from each other in ways that matter to some people and not at all to others. Anyone trying to stay current isn't really catching up to a destination. They're catching up to a direction.

That's the feeling this essay keeps circling, the one the title is trying to hold onto. Not behind, exactly. Not ahead either. Just perpetually in the process of arriving somewhere that keeps redrawing itself slightly further out, right as you get close.

Maybe that's simply what it means to work in something this alive right now. Not a problem to be solved, not a pace to be matched — just the actual shape of being here, at this particular moment, watching a field that refuses to sit still long enough to be fully known.

Almost there, always.
